About Me

My Journey to Educational Research

Unlocking Human Potential Through Learning Science

Watching my younger brother and sister Sarah struggle with learning (despite their intelligence) made me question why some students seem to absorb information effortlessly while others work twice as hard for half the results. I became obsessed with memory and learning, attempting to memorize vast amounts of information in seconds, frustrated that I didn't have photographic memory like some people seemed to possess.

This frustration led me down a transformative path. I discovered memory palaces, mnemonic techniques, and spaced repetition systems. More importantly, I learned that "photographic memory" isn't innate. It's about leveraging how our brains naturally process and retain information. The revelation was profound: learning isn't about natural talent or fixed ability. It's about understanding the cognitive architecture of memory, attention, and comprehension, then designing environments and tools that work with our brain's mechanisms rather than against them.

This insight transformed my entire approach to education. My research now focuses on unlocking learning potential for all students, especially those who (like my siblings) face barriers that have nothing to do with their capability. By integrating cognitive science, developmental psychology, and educational technology, I'm working to make learning more accessible, efficient, and equitable. Every child deserves tools and environments designed around how humans actually learn, not outdated assumptions about who can and cannot succeed.

My Mission

I'm dedicated to democratizing effective learning by bridging cognitive science, educational technology, and human development. My research explores how we can design learning environments and assessment tools that align with how our brains naturally process information, making education more accessible, efficient, and equitable for all learners.

Through automated reading assessments, accessible learning platforms, and evidence-based interventions, I work to unlock each student's potential by removing artificial barriers and leveraging insights from memory science, cognitive load theory, and developmental psychology. Every learner deserves tools designed around human cognition, not outdated assumptions about ability.

Research Agenda

Core Research Questions

Reading Development & Assessment

How do children develop reading fluency and comprehension? Investigating automated oral reading assessments that capture developmental trajectories while integrating cognitive models of reading acquisition and educational measurement principles to reduce teacher burden.

Cognitive Load & Digital Learning

How do interface design decisions in digital texts affect cognitive load and comprehension strategy development? Examining how learners navigate multimodal content, particularly those who rely on screen readers or operate in multilingual contexts.

Learning Sciences & Accessibility

How can learning sciences principles and Universal Design for Learning be operationalized within adaptive platforms? Exploring how to reduce extraneous cognitive load while supporting the development of academic language and self-regulated learning strategies.

Current Research at READS Lab

Developing automated reading fluency assessment systems that analyze classroom audio recordings using advanced speech processing. Working with teacher and student recordings, I fine-tune speech-to-text models (Whisper, Google Speech-to-Text) and build end-to-end pipelines that convert audio to text, then transform speech into vector representations. These embeddings capture prosodic features, emotional tone, sentiment patterns, and vocabulary networks across different grade levels. By analyzing how these linguistic and affective dimensions evolve developmentally, the system identifies reading fluency indicators while reducing manual coding by 60% and maintaining 90%+ accuracy. Additionally conducting causal inference analyses (difference-in-differences, propensity score matching) to evaluate how vocabulary and background knowledge interventions influence comprehension trajectories.
